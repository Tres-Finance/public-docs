# From Fragmented Derivatives Data to a Single Source of Truth

Derivatives are now the primary liquidity engine for digital assets, with perpetual contracts account for *78% of total crypto derivatives. They help institutions hedge exposure, execute trading strategies, and do more with less capital. Without them, market volatility would be far greater, and liquidity, more limited.

But the ecosystem has evolved fast. What once happened on centralized exchanges like Binance and Bybit, has now expanded into a broader ecosystem. Decentralized venues such as HyperLiquid and broker-dealer platforms have entered the market, each with its own data structure and reporting rules. Liquidity is more flexible than ever, but the result is fragmented data: trades, positions, and settlements that don’t line up – making reconciliation and accounting far more complex.

Meanwhile, regulatory expectations have surged. Finance teams must classify every funding payment, track every micro-settlement, and justify every P&L movement – with audit-ready precision.

In this article, we explore the challenges of digital asset derivatives accounting and reconciliation, and how to solve it.

### The challenge: fragmented data

Derivatives trading data is one of the most complex parts of digital asset finance. Every exchange structures and reports its data differently, hiding a lot of complexity beneath the surface.

Unlike blockchain transactions, which follow a clear and universal standard, derivatives data is a matter of interpretation. The same event can appear in completely different formats depending on the platform.

For example:

- Funding fees may be aggregated per asset on one exchange but per position on another

- Settlements may appear as individual microtransactions or as daily summaries

- Transaction labels and fee categories vary widely across venues

For participants operating across multiple exchanges or liquidity venues, this fragmentation makes reconciliation a constant challenge. What should be a unified ledger becomes a mix of incompatible data streams that require aggregation, interpretation, and simplification before they can even be read accurately.

The challenge is not trading derivatives but making sense of them afterward.
